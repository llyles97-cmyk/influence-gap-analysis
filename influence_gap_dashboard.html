Mean Influence Gap Score by platform

The gap is structural — a product of platform architecture, not creator quality

Platform IGS: Twitter −0.41, Instagram −0.07, YouTube +0.04, TikTok +0.33.
TikTok (+0.33): Algorithmic distribution by interest, not follower count. Content reaches high-intent audiences who didn't choose to follow — behavioral action follows.

Twitter (−0.44): Optimized for retweets, replies, and discourse. Platform rewards interaction loudly; purchase intent rarely follows the conversation.

YouTube (+0.04): Search-intent discovery drives longer consideration cycles — lower engagement, but higher-quality conversion when it happens.

Creators don't fail platforms. Platforms fail brands.

Seven creator archetypes — ranked by mean IGS

Bar width = share of dataset  ·  IGS and CVR per segment

68% of campaigns are Spike Artists — near-zero IGS, high variance. The default state of influencer marketing: neither reliably good nor reliably bad.

Quiet Converters are only 1.3% of campaigns but deliver the highest action yield in the dataset — systematically underpriced because their engagement metrics look unremarkable.

Engagement Traps (1.6%) carry a mean IGS of −5.07 — the most extreme gap in the dataset. Brands paying on engagement metrics are funding this segment disproportionately.

Pearson r values are statistically significant (p < 0.001) across all platforms — but statistical significance and practical significance are not the same thing. Every creator brief built on engagement rate as a primary selection signal is optimizing for noise.
